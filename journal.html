The funnel was always a metaphor.

Author Eloísa Marçal Volume XI · No. 04 Reading time ≈ 7 min

We borrowed it from physics, then drew it badly. A real funnel narrows what passes through to a specific outlet. A marketing funnel was supposed to describe how strangers became customers — but somewhere in the last decade it became the entire vocabulary, then the entire operating manual, then the entire strategy.

The result is the marketing many of you live inside: bid prices that never settle, creative testing that never compounds, attribution that doesn’t agree with revenue. The funnel narrative implies the work is to widen the top — to find more strangers — but the brands we know best win on the other end of the sentence: by being the kind of presence people end up at without having to be sent.

“Brands are like that: gravitational. The work, then, is not to chase but to be — and the practice we run is the slow construction of that mass.”

We use the word field for that. It is not a clever name. It is what physics calls a region of space within which an object exerts force on the things inside it. Brands are like that: gravitational. The work, then, is not to chase but to be — and the practice we run is the slow construction of that mass. We are willing to be wrong about this in public. We are very rarely wrong in private.

A practical implication: the org chart most marketing functions run on was designed for the funnel. If you stay inside the metaphor, you’ll keep buying the same paid-acquisition machinery, the same brief, the same kind of agency. If you leave it, the machinery has to change too — different hires, different cadences, different KPI shape. The reason most teams don’t leave the metaphor isn’t intellectual disagreement; it’s that the change of operating model is genuinely uncomfortable.

We’ve found that the brands willing to make the change tend to share three things: a principal who reads, a willingness to lose a quarter to get a year, and a CFO who has already privately stopped trusting paid-acquisition reporting. Those are the engagements we run for. The rest, with respect, are someone else’s.

— Eloísa Marçal · Lisbon, June ’26
