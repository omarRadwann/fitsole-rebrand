ALCOVE 01 / 03 cardinal orchard · B2B SaaS · contract analytics

Cardinal Orchard

B2B SaaS · contract analytics · ~$28M ARR
Context

A contract-analytics platform with strong product fit and a marketing site full of "let's chat." Two pricing tiers existed; neither was visible to a prospect before a sales call. Sales cycle median was 14 weeks; quarterly review was the third one in a row asking why.

Diagnostic finding

Money-on-the-page absent on every plan. Spread test ran 71 mm on "full-funnel campaigns" — the page promised a system the team did not own. The studio published 0 refusals.

Shipped

A three-tier pricing page with prices, plan caps, and three public refusals ("we don't sell month-to-month; we don't do PoVs; we don't take enterprises smaller than $50M ARR"). Eleven days from kick-off to live.

Δ

Sales cycle: 14 → 11 weeks median.
Qualified-demo rate: +38%.
Both held into the following quarter.

Drag the jaw — reveal mechanism

The mechanism wasn't the page. It was the pricing rationale we wrote to accompany it — a 600-word internal note about why each tier was priced where it was, and what the refusal language was for. Sales started quoting the rationale verbatim in the first call; the prospects who would have argued price arguing instead about whether the refusals applied to them. The page disqualified the wrong fits before sales had to.

ALCOVE 02 / 03 oblique stack · developer tool · CI/CD

Oblique Stack

Developer tool · CI/CD · ~$14M ARR
Context

A CI/CD tool with a strong developer following and a sign-up sequence that had been copied from another company's template four years prior. Eight welcome emails arrived in the first two weeks. The team had measured open rate; they had not measured information rate.

Diagnostic finding

Lifecycle redundancy at 0.375 — eight emails, three carrying distinct information, five repeating the welcome. Activation rate had been declining for six months even as opens climbed.

Shipped

A four-email sequence keyed to actual repository activity. Each email reports something the user has done in the previous 48 hours: first push, first failed build, first parallel-job run, first protected-branch policy. No marketing email; only operational reflection.

Δ

Activation: +44% within four weeks.
Unsubscribes: -2 per 1,000.
A reduction in the sequence improved both axes.

Drag the jaw — reveal mechanism

The mechanism was timing, not content. The four emails would have failed if sent on a fixed schedule. The trigger was repository activity, which meant the team had to ship an integration with the product before they could ship the sequence. We held the sequence build until the integration was live; otherwise we'd have shipped a fifth piece of theatre. The "marketing" win required a "product" prerequisite, and we said so in writing before we started.

ALCOVE 03 / 03 terrace & vault · fintech · embedded payments

Terrace & Vault

Fintech · embedded payments · ~$45M ARR
Context

An embedded-payments fintech with a fast product team and a slow decision-velocity score. Every meaningful marketing decision routed through legal review; legal review averaged 14 weeks on positioning copy. Three positioning bets had died in the queue in the previous year.

Diagnostic finding

Decision velocity at 14 weeks median, 22 weeks slowest, all stuck in legal review. The marketing team was outputting at a third of its rated capacity because so little ever cleared. Attribution honesty also scored low at 2 / 5.

Shipped

A positioning document with a pre-cleared legal-language appendix. Sales can quote any of the seven approved framings without re-routing. The appendix itself took three weeks; everything written using it shipped same-week thereafter.

Δ

Median decision velocity: 14 → 3 weeks.
Three pending positioning bets shipped in the next month.
Legal-review hours fell as a side-effect; nobody had measured them before.

Drag the jaw — reveal mechanism

The mechanism was a permission contract, not a faster legal team. The appendix was an explicit, written list of the language the marketing team was pre-authorised to use, signed by the GC. Anything outside it still routed through legal. The point was not to skip the lawyers; it was to define the shape of language the lawyers had already pre-approved. The legal team liked it because the bulk of their review work disappeared; the marketing team liked it because they could ship without asking permission for things permission had already been given for.
