# 65 — SOTY / SOTD Patterns Master

Patterns observed across Site of the Year and Site of the Day winners. Use these as a sanity check on the concept — not as a recipe to copy.

## What SOTY winners share

1. **A single legible idea**, expressible in one sentence by a stranger after 30 seconds on the site.
2. **A signature first frame** — a screenshot that could be the brand's identity post.
3. **Concept-tied interaction** — the one thing the site does that no other site does the same way.
4. **Restraint** — at least one deliberate "we chose not to" decision visible in the work.
5. **Mobile equivalence** — the mobile version is *its own design*, not a stack.
6. **Performance feel** — site feels light even when it's technically heavy.
7. **Content commitment** — copy and imagery feel produced for this project, not pulled in.

A site missing any one of those is almost never SOTY material, regardless of polish.

## SOTD patterns by category

### Brand / portfolio sites
- Editorial typography as the primary visual.
- Long hero with one moment of motion.
- Case studies presented as documents, not slideshows.
- Strong color used sparingly.

### SaaS / product sites
- Product mechanic shown, not described.
- One illustrated/animated diagram instead of three feature cards.
- Pricing or process exposed transparently.
- A founder/team moment that breaks the SaaS template.

### Agency / studio sites
- Self-aware tone in the copy.
- Selected work over comprehensive list.
- Process explained with specificity.
- Signature visual motif that runs through the whole site.

### Local service / professional
- Calm, confident hero (not stock photos of smiling staff).
- Real proof: process, materials, before/after when honest.
- Clear, present CTA (call, book, WhatsApp).
- Location and service area made obvious.

### E-commerce
- Product treated as an object worth photographing well.
- Editorial story alongside the buy.
- Material/process/origin content where relevant.
- Cart that feels designed, not bolted on.

### Immersive / cinematic / experimental
- One concept clearly worth the technical cost.
- Loading state designed as part of the experience.
- A "way out" — accessible content underneath the experience.
- Audio that can be muted by default and felt when on.

## SOTD anti-patterns

If the site has any of these, it is not SOTD-ready:

- Hero headline that could describe any company.
- Three centered feature cards with generic icons.
- Background gradient as the entire personality.
- Random 3D object unrelated to the offer.
- Scroll-jacking with no payoff.
- Mobile is a vertical stack of desktop sections.
- "Trusted by" row with logos the user can't verify.
- Founder/team photos that look generated.
- Stock illustration that doesn't match the type system.
- Fake dashboard / fake metrics.

## The "first 5 seconds" SOTD test

1. Land on the site.
2. After 5 seconds, close the tab.
3. Write down: what the business is, who it's for, and what the next action is.

If you can't, the site won't survive jury attention either.

## The "memory" test (one week)

A SOTY winner is remembered a week later by one specific visual or interaction. Before declaring a site finished, define which **single thing** you expect the visitor to remember. If you can't, the site isn't ready.

## The bravery question

Every SOTY winner has at least one decision that the average client would have rejected. Identify yours. If there isn't one, the concept is too safe.

Examples of brave decisions that paid off:
- A type-only hero with no imagery.
- A monochrome palette on a category that defaults to color.
- A scroll story that takes 45 seconds to read instead of 10.
- A first frame that doesn't include the company name.
- A CTA in plain text in the middle of a sentence.

## Where to put this in the workflow

- During concept competition, score each concept against the SOTY shared traits.
- During visual red-team, run the "first 5 seconds" and "memory" tests.
- Before ship, name the brave decision and the one-week memory in `docs/ship-decision.md`.

If those answers are weak, the site is shippable but not award-capable. Be honest about which one you're delivering.
