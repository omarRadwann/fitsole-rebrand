# 67 — Award-Winning Case Study Patterns

How portfolio / agency / studio sites present case studies on award-winning sites, and how to apply the patterns to one-page hero sections, project showcases, and proof galleries.

## What jurors look for in a case study presentation

1. **Specificity** — the case study reads like only this team could have written it.
2. **Mechanism over outcome** — *how* they solved it, not just that they did.
3. **Visual sequencing** — the work is shown in a deliberate order, not a screenshot dump.
4. **Voice** — first-person where it earns trust; third-person where it earns objectivity.
5. **Restraint** — the case study doesn't try to show everything.

## Case study archetypes

### The Document
Long-form, editorial layout, image-text rhythm, generous type. Works for design-led agencies and senior studios. Feels like reading a magazine article.

### The Reel
Image/video-first, sparse text, designed to be skimmed in 20 seconds. Works for motion-heavy or visually-driven work where the imagery does the persuasion.

### The Build Log
Step-by-step process visible — sketches, iterations, code snippets, technical decisions. Works for engineering-led studios and developer-award candidates.

### The Outcome
Numbers and proof up front, work shown as evidence afterward. Works for performance-driven agencies (CRO, growth, e-commerce) where the result is the headline.

### The Diorama
Each project gets a unique micro-site or section design that matches the project's own tone. High effort, high impact, easy to get wrong. Reserved for hero pieces.

## The skeleton (use as a starting point, then break it)

```
1. Project name + client + year + role (one line)
2. The pitch: one sentence, what we made and why it matters
3. The problem: in the client's own language, not yours
4. The constraint: what made this hard
5. The decision: the bet we made
6. The mechanism: how it works, with one diagram or sequence
7. The visual moment: the screenshot a stranger could remember
8. The detail: a craft moment that proves the rigor
9. The outcome: honest, specific, measurable if possible
10. The credit: full team, roles
```

Not every case study uses all 10. The strongest ones often use 5–7 and use them well.

## Header pattern for case study cards

Avoid the generic "Client / Industry / Year" triple. Replace with a structured tease that hints at the bet:

```
Architectural Studio · 2025
Their first website that explains the practice without showing the projects.
```

The second line is the pitch. It rewards a hover or a click in a way "View case study →" never will.

## Sequencing the visuals

Order the case study by **information flow**, not by chronology:

1. The strongest single visual (lead).
2. Context — what the surrounding work looks like.
3. The mechanism in motion (animation or interactive demo).
4. Detail crops — type, micro-interactions, UI specifics.
5. Counterfactual — what you rejected and why.
6. Outcome — quietly.

Chronological order ("here's the wireframe, here's the design, here's the launch") is the most common case-study mistake.

## Copy patterns that age well

- **First-person plural with specificity**: "We wanted the homepage to feel like the first 30 seconds of a documentary." Not "We pushed boundaries."
- **Honest constraint naming**: "The client gave us four weeks and one round of revisions." Not "Within tight timelines."
- **Mechanism description**: "The hero is a single 3D scene rendered with R3F, with a poster fallback under 350KB." Not "A custom WebGL experience."

## Copy patterns to avoid

- "We are proud to have partnered with..."
- "Pushing the boundaries of digital..."
- "A bespoke, fully-custom solution..."
- Hero metrics with no methodology ("+312% engagement").
- Generic praise quotes ("Working with them was a dream.").

## Case studies in a one-page hero

When the site itself is the case-study showcase (an agency or studio site), the hero must:

1. Name the kind of work you take, in the user's words.
2. Tease one specific recent project with a fragment, not a thumbnail of everything.
3. Give the visitor one path to either selected work or a brief.

Three featured projects, presented as documents, beat twelve thumbnails every time.

## The proof-without-proof case

When a case study cannot share outcomes (NDA, early-stage, sensitive client):

- Replace "result" with **role**: what we owned and how.
- Replace "metrics" with **method**: the approach, in detail.
- Replace "client quote" with **founder note**: your own honest reflection on the project.

This is more interesting than fake numbers and more defensible than vague metrics.

## Honesty rule

Never invent metrics. Never invent quotes. Never present an experimental build as a launched product. Mark proof-of-concepts as such. The award jury has seen everything; they will spot it. The user will spot it eventually too.
