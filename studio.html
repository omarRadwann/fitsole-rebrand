Nine pairs of hands, two cities, one operating thesis.

Halo is a growth-marketing practice built around a single contrarian idea: brands are gravitational, not promotional. We don’t take on creative briefs. We take on operating mandates — typically six to thirty months — and we run them out of two studios with the same nine hands that started the firm.

The thesis

Brand is the only compound interest left in marketing.

In 2014 we left a holding-co agency convinced that the funnel metaphor was eating the discipline. Ten years in, every measurement system we’ve seen confirms it: paid acquisition is a deteriorating curve; owned audience is the only line that compounds; brand presence is the lone moat that doesn’t depend on ad-platform whims.

We don’t think this is a hot take. We think it’s the boring observation that most CFOs already privately make — and that most CMOs are afraid to operate on, because the alternative (a slower, more deliberate, evidence-led practice) is genuinely harder to staff for.

So we built one. Nine partners who have run growth at brands you’ve heard of, working out of two cities, on a deliberately small mandate count. We exist to be the operating partner founders and boards want their marketing function to behave like.

How we choose engagements

A practice with a mandate, not a roster.

We take three new engagements a year. We turn down most of the inbound that reaches us. The bar isn’t prestige or budget — it’s whether the engagement can produce an outcome we’d be willing to write about in the next annual letter.

01 · The Why

There has to be a real shift on the table.

A category move, a positioning rewrite, an architectural change in how the brand goes to market. We’re uninteresting on incremental campaign work; the practice doesn’t set up well for it.

02 · The Time

Twelve months minimum.

Strategy is the only compound interest left in marketing. We can’t ship a shift in a quarter, and we’re uncomfortable being paid as if we could.

03 · The Founder

A principal in the room.

Every engagement has a weekly call with a founder, CEO, or managing partner. We don’t take on work routed only through a marketing director — the work doesn’t survive translation.

04 · The Product

An actual product, not a vehicle.

The brand must stand for something the firm is willing to defend in public. If the product is a thin wrapper or the brand is a financial instrument, we’re not the right partner.

05 · The Math

Numbers that can actually change.

We want measurement we can run for and against. If the only available metric is impressions, the engagement is wrong for us; we work where CAC, AOV, share-of-voice, and pipeline are real.

06 · The Calendar

Capacity, not just appetite.

We hold the book at seven concurrent engagements. New mandates start when one closes — sometimes that means a six-month wait, and we’d rather be honest about it than dilute the practice.

What we won’t do

Some refusals, on the record.

A practice is mostly the work it turns away. We publish the list so prospective clients can decide if the relationship fits before either of us writes a brief.

  • No. 01

    Scaling ad spend without a strategy.

    We don’t take performance-marketing engagements without an accompanying brand or positioning mandate. If the only thing on the table is "spend more in Meta", we are the wrong vendor and the cheaper option is correct.

  • No. 02

    Creative direction for products we believe are dishonest.

    Quietly, in 2019, we instituted a partner vote on every new engagement. The bar is whether all nine of us would be comfortable being publicly associated with the brand. We’ve said no to fourteen mandates on this basis since.

  • No. 03

    Velocity-as-a-service.

    We don’t take "fractional CMO" arrangements or burst-capacity retainers where the assumption is that we’re a velocity multiplier on an existing operating model. Either we’re running the function or we’re not.

  • No. 04

    Anything with a relaunch deadline shorter than the rationale needs.

    We will refuse a 6-week brand rebuild commissioned because a new website goes live in 8 weeks. We’ve made this mistake twice; both times the work was the worst of our year.

Operating principles

The five we defend in public.

The expanded version of what gets compressed onto the home page. We rewrite this list once a year and it has changed shape three times since 2014.

  1. Strategy is the only compound interest left in marketing.

    The decade of cheap paid acquisition is over and most of the techniques the discipline still teaches are optimised for a world that no longer exists. Without a strategy, paid spend is expensive guessing. We build for the brands willing to invest in the slower thing.

  2. Brand is a system, not a logo.

    A brand is the whole field of how strangers come to a decision about you — the surfaces, the language, the editorial rhythm, the public arguments your partners are willing to make. We design the system end-to-end and we’re uninterested in any sub-component of it on its own.

  3. Evidence beats opinion.

    Every claim we make, every brief we write, every dashboard we ship ties back to a measurable shift. We’re not opposed to taste — we run a practice in two of the most stylish cities in the world — but we are opposed to confusing taste with argument.

  4. Editorial beats interruption.

    Owned audiences compound; rented ones evaporate. The work that survives a CAC inflation, a privacy update, or a platform de-ranking is almost always editorial: things you wrote, said, or did under your own roof. We optimise for that even when it’s slower in the first quarter.

  5. Quarterly thinking beats sprint thinking.

    Most marketing organisations run on a sprint cadence inherited from product. We run on a quarterly one. Every 90 days the engagement brief is interrogated against the data and either doubled, recalibrated, or quietly cut. We commit to the long arc; we don’t commit to last quarter’s wrong call.
